## Grant Management Philosophy

### Foundation's Grant Strategy

**Mission-Driven Funding**: Pursue grants that advance AATD patient advocacy, research, education, or supportâ€”never chase money that diverts from mission.

**Diversified Portfolio**: Balance grant types:
- **Foundation grants** (30-40%): Private foundations, family foundations
- **Corporate grants** (20-30%): Pharmaceutical companies, healthcare corporations
- **Government grants** (20-30%): NIH, CDC, HRSA, state health departments
- **Individual major gifts** (10-20%): High-net-worth donors making 5-6 figure gifts

**Sustainability Focus**: Avoid over-reliance on single funder (no funder >25% of budgetâ€”reduces risk if they exit).

**Capacity Matching**: Only apply for grants Foundation can successfully execute (don't overcommit, underdeliver).

**Long-Term Relationships**: Treat grant as beginning of relationship, not transactionâ€”multi-year support, partnerships, advocacy allies.

---

### Grant Lifecycle Stages

**7 Stages** (each covered in detail below):

1. **Prospecting** (3-6 months): Identify potential funders, research fit
2. **Cultivation** (1-3 months): Build relationships, educate funders about AATD
3. **Proposal Development** (1-2 months): Write LOI or full proposal
4. **Submission** (1 day): Submit on time, confirm receipt
5. **Award & Negotiation** (1-3 months): Review terms, negotiate if needed, accept
6. **Performance** (1-3 years): Execute program, report progress, manage budget
7. **Closeout** (1-2 months): Final reports, financial reconciliation, stewardship

**Total Timeline**: 6 months (prospecting) â†’ 3 years (multi-year grant) â†’ 2 months (closeout) = **~4 years per grant cycle**.

## Letter of Inquiry (LOI) Development

### When LOI Required

**Purpose**: Screening toolâ€”funder reviews 100 LOIs, invites 20-30 to submit full proposals (saves everyone time).

**Typical Length**: 2-3 pages, 1,000-1,500 words

**Components**:
1. **Organization Overview** (1 paragraph): Mission, history, credibility
2. **Problem Statement** (1 paragraph): Why AATD patients need this program
3. **Proposed Solution** (1-2 paragraphs): What Foundation will do, how it addresses problem
4. **Expected Outcomes** (1 paragraph): Measurable impact (# patients served, % improvement)
5. **Budget Summary** (1 sentence): Total project cost, amount requested from funder
6. **Organizational Capacity** (1 paragraph): Why Foundation can deliver (track record, expertise)

---

### LOI Writing Best Practices

**Lead with Impact**: Start with compelling patient story or statistic ("75% of AATD patients undiagnosedâ€”leading to preventable lung damage").

**Be Specific**: Avoid jargon, vague goals. Instead of "improve patient outcomes," say "increase diagnostic rate by 20% through 500 physician education webinars."

**Demonstrate Need**: Use data (patient surveys, epidemiology studies, gaps in current services).

**Show Capacity**: Mention key staff qualifications, partnerships, past successes ("Our diagnosis program identified 200 new patients in Year 1").

**Match Funder Priorities**: Echo funder's language ("Your foundation prioritizes health equityâ€”our program targets underserved rural communities").

**Proofread**: Typos signal sloppiness. Have 2 people review before submitting.

---

### LOI Submission & Follow-Up

**Submission**:
- Submit via funder's online portal (most common) OR email if no portal
- Confirm receipt within 24 hours (email or phone)
- Log in CRM: Date submitted, amount requested, decision timeline

**Follow-Up**:
- If invited to full proposal: Celebrate, start drafting immediately (usually 30-60 day deadline)
- If declined: Email thank-you, ask for feedback ("We'd appreciate any suggestions for future submissions"), maintain relationship
- If no response: Follow up after stated timeline + 2 weeks ("Checking status of our LOI submitted [date]")

**Invite Rate**: 20-30% of LOIs invited to full proposal (strong LOI can increase to 40-50%).

## Full Proposal Writing Process

### Proposal Components

**Standard Grant Proposal** (10-20 pages):

1. **Cover Letter** (1 page): Personalized, signed by ED, summarizes request
2. **Executive Summary** (1 page): Condensed version (organization, project, budget, impact)
3. **Organizational Overview** (2 pages): History, mission, programs, governance, financials
4. **Problem/Need Statement** (2-3 pages): Document the problem (AATD burden, gaps in care)
5. **Program Description** (3-5 pages): Goals, activities, timeline, staffing, partnerships
6. **Evaluation Plan** (2 pages): How success measured (metrics, data collection, reporting)
7. **Budget & Justification** (2 pages): Detailed budget, narrative explaining costs
8. **Sustainability Plan** (1 page): How program continues after grant ends
9. **Appendices**: 990, audited financials, Board list, letters of support

---

### Writing Assignments

**Collaborative Process**:

**Grants Manager**: Leads writing, coordinates team
**Program Staff**: Write program description, evaluation plan (they know program details)
**Finance**: Create budget, budget narrative
**ED/Chief Development Officer**: Write organizational overview, cover letter
**External Evaluator** (if applicable): Design evaluation, contribute evaluation section

**Timeline**: 4-6 weeks from LOI invitation to submission deadline (don't wait until last minute).

---

### Proposal Writing Best Practices

**SMART Goals**: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound
- âŒ Weak: "Improve patient quality of life"
- âœ… Strong: "Increase patient knowledge of AATD self-management by 30% (pre/post-test) among 200 support group participants within 18 months"

**Logic Model**: Show how activities â†’ outputs â†’ outcomes â†’ impact
- **Activities**: Education webinars
- **Outputs**: 500 clinicians trained
- **Outcomes**: Clinicians order more diagnostic tests
- **Impact**: 100 additional AATD patients diagnosed, receive treatment, avoid complications

**Evidence-Based**: Cite research, pilot data, peer models
- "Our pilot program in Ohio diagnosed 50 patients in 6 monthsâ€”we're ready to scale nationally."
- "Studies show genetic counseling increases testing uptake by 40% (Smith et al. 2022, AJRCCM)."

**Storytelling**: Weave patient stories throughout (humanizes data)
- "Maria, age 45, struggled with shortness of breath for 10 years before our program identified her AATD. Now on augmentation therapy, she's back to hiking with her kids."

**Address Potential Concerns**: Anticipate funder questions
- If program new: "While this program is new, our team has 50+ years combined experience in rare disease advocacy."
- If budget high: "Cost per patient ($500) is far less than cost of one COPD hospitalization ($15,000)â€”this is prevention investment."

**Visuals**: Include charts, photos, infographics (break up text, improve readability)
- Program timeline (Gantt chart)
- Impact dashboard (# patients served, satisfaction %)
- Photos of patients, support groups, events (with photo releases)

## Award Negotiation & Acceptance

### Award Notification

**Congratulations Call**: Funder calls or emails: "We're pleased to award $200,000 for your Patient Navigation Program."

**Immediate Response**:
- **Thank funder** (verbally and in writing within 24 hours)
- **Confirm acceptance** (pending grant agreement reviewâ€”see next section)
- **Ask clarifying questions**: Payment schedule, reporting requirements, site visits planned?

---

### Negotiation Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Partial Funding**
- **Funder says**: "We're awarding $150,000 instead of $200,000 requested."
- **Foundation options**:
  - Accept and scale program (serve 150 patients instead of 200)
  - Accept and find co-funders (apply to another funder for remaining $50K)
  - Decline if program not viable at reduced funding (rare, but ethical if you can't deliver)

**Scenario 2: Restricted Use**
- **Funder says**: "We'll fund salaries but not indirect costs."
- **Foundation response**: Negotiate compromise ("Could you fund 50% indirect? We need to cover rent, utilities for program space") OR accept and budget indirect from unrestricted funds

**Scenario 3: Timeline Changes**
- **Funder says**: "We can't start funding until July (3 months later than you requested)."
- **Foundation response**: Adjust program timeline, delay hiring, or use bridge funding (line of credit, reserves) to start on time

**Red Flags** (consider declining):
- âŒ Funder requires rights to all program data, IP (threatens patient privacy, organizational autonomy)
- âŒ Funder demands Board seat (conflicts of interest, governance issues)
- âŒ Funding contingent on unethical activities (promoting funder's product over patient needs)

## Grant Agreement Review

### Legal Review Process

**Before Signing**, have attorney review if:
- Grant >$100,000 (large financial commitment)
- Unusual terms (IP rights, data ownership, naming rights)
- Multi-year commitment (legal obligations beyond current fiscal year)
- New funder (no track record, uncertain reputation)

**Attorney Review Cost**: $500-$2,000 (worth investment to avoid costly mistakes).

---

### Key Contract Terms to Scrutinize

**1. Payment Schedule**:
- **Upfront**: Funder pays full amount at grant start (best for cash flow)
- **Quarterly**: Funder pays 25% every 3 months (requires cash flow management)
- **Reimbursement**: Foundation spends first, submits invoices, funder reimburses 30-60 days later (strains cash flowâ€”may need line of credit)

**2. Reporting Requirements**:
- **Frequency**: Monthly, quarterly, annually (monthly is burdensome for small staff)
- **Format**: Narrative + financials (standard), site visits (time-intensive), data dashboards (requires tech infrastructure)
- **Deliverables**: Specific outputs required (# patients served, # clinicians trained)

**3. Budget Flexibility**:
- **Line-item budget**: Can't move >10% between categories without funder approval (restrictive)
- **Categorical budget**: Flexibility within personnel, program, indirect (preferred)
- **No-cost extensions**: Can Foundation request extra time to spend funds if program delayed? (Most funders allow one 6-12 month extension)

**4. Intellectual Property**:
- **Foundation owns**: Curriculum, data, publications (standard)
- **Joint ownership**: If funder co-develops (negotiate carefully)
- **Funder owns**: Red flag (Foundation invests time, funder profits)

**5. Termination Clauses**:
- **For cause**: Funder can terminate if Foundation breaches contract (fraud, noncomplianceâ€”reasonable)
- **For convenience**: Funder can terminate anytime without cause (riskyâ€”means mid-year funding loss)
- **Repayment**: If terminated, must Foundation repay unspent funds? (Yesâ€”standard) Spent funds? (Noâ€”unless fraud)

**6. Publicity & Recognition**:
- **Logo use**: Funder logo on materials, website, events (standard)
- **Press releases**: Joint announcements (coordinate messaging)
- **Naming rights**: "ABC Foundation AATD Patient Navigation Program" (acceptable if not in perpetuity)

## Financial Management & Drawdowns

### Grant Accounting

**Separate Tracking** (per grant):
- Each grant = separate project code in accounting software (QuickBooks, Sage Intacct)
- All expenses charged to grant coded with project code
- Separate bank account NOT required (but some funders preferâ€”ask)

**Restricted Funds Accounting**:
- Grant revenue recorded as **temporarily restricted net assets** (GAAP requirement)
- As funds spent, **released from restriction** to unrestricted (matches expenses)
- Ensures grants used only for intended purpose

---

### Budget Monitoring

**Monthly Review** (Grants Manager + Finance Director):
- Compare actual expenses to grant budget
- **Variance Analysis**: Why did we spend $12,000 on travel when budget was $10,000?
  - Acceptable variance: Unforeseen conference opportunity, advance funder approval
  - Unacceptable variance: Poor budget planning, spending without approval
- **Reforecasting**: If budget off-track, reforecast remaining year, identify corrective action

**Red Flags**:
- Spending too fast (50% of budget spent in first 25% of grant periodâ€”will run out of money)
- Spending too slow (10% spent halfway through grantâ€”program not launching, funder may reclaim funds)
- Over-budget categories (need funder approval to reallocate)

---

### Drawdown Procedures

**Reimbursement-Based Grants**:
1. **Incur Expenses**: Foundation pays vendors, employees
2. **Compile Invoices**: Monthly or quarterly, gather receipts, timesheets
3. **Submit Drawdown Request**: Invoice packet to funder (cover letter, detailed expense report, supporting docs)
4. **Funder Review**: 30-60 day review period
5. **Payment**: Check or ACH transfer
6. **Cash Flow Gap**: Foundation must bridge (line of credit, reservesâ€”see Cash Flow Management in Budget Development Process document)

**Advance Payment Grants**:
1. **Grant Starts**: Funder sends first payment (25-100% of grant)
2. **Foundation Spends**: Pay expenses as incurred

**Best Practice**: Track drawdowns in separate spreadsheet (date requested, amount, date received, variance from expected).

## Grant Closeout Procedures

### Final Reporting

**Final Report Due** (typically 30-60 days after grant end date):

**Content**:
- **Full Grant Period Summary**: All activities, outputs, outcomes (cumulative)
- **Goals Met**: Compare actual to proposed (Did you serve 200 patients as promised? If not, why?)
- **Lessons Learned**: Candid reflection (What worked? What didn't? What would you do differently?)
- **Sustainability Update**: Program continuing? New funders secured? Program ended?
- **Final Financials**: Total spent, budget vs. actual, any unspent funds

**Unspent Funds**:
- If grant allows carryover, request extension (spend in Year 2)
- If not, return to funder (check with amount)
- Never spend unspent funds on non-grant activities without funder permission (fraud)

---

### Financial Reconciliation

**Final Financial Report**:
- **Total Revenue Received**: All payments from funder
- **Total Expenses Incurred**: All project costs (charged to grant project code)
- **Variance**: Should be near zero (within 1-2%)
  - If under-budget: Explain why (cost savings, program completed for less)
  - If over-budget: Explain how gap funded (Foundation match increased, other funder covered overrun)

**Audit Trail**: Keep all receipts, invoices, timesheets, financial reports for **7 years** (IRS retention requirement, many funders require 5-7 years).

## Funder Relationship Management

### Building Long-Term Partnerships

**Goal**: Convert one-time funders to multi-year partners.

**Strategies**:

**1. Over-Communicate**: Share updates beyond required reports (quarterly e-newsletters, patient stories, event invites).

**2. Involve Funders**: Invite to site visits, program observations, Board presentations (builds investment, ownership).

**3. Recognize Publicly**: Thank funders in annual report, website, social media (most funders appreciate recognitionâ€”check if they prefer anonymity).

**4. Demonstrate Impact**: Use data, stories, visuals to show how their dollars made difference (funders want to see ROI).

**5. Seek Feedback**: Ask "How can we improve our reporting? What information is most useful to you?" (shows partnership, not transactional relationship).

**6. Invite Renewal**: 6 months before grant ends, approach funder about Year 2 funding ("Would you consider continuing your support? Here's our proposed planâ€¦").

---

### Managing Difficult Funders

**Scenario 1: Micromanaging Funder**
- **Symptom**: Weekly emails asking for updates, frequent calls, excessive reporting requests
- **Response**: Set boundaries politely ("We'll provide quarterly reports as agreedâ€”happy to schedule check-in calls each quarter if helpful"), loop in ED if escalates

**Scenario 2: Slow-Paying Funder**
- **Symptom**: Payment 90 days overdue, affecting Foundation cash flow
- **Response**: Polite reminder emails (escalate to funder's CFO if necessary), pause program if payment critical, consider not reapplying if chronic issue

**Scenario 3: Scope Creep**
- **Symptom**: Funder asks Foundation to add activities not in grant proposal ("Can you also do genetic testing?" when grant was for education only)
- **Response**: "We'd love to, but that's beyond current grant scope. Would you consider supplemental funding to expand?" OR "Let's discuss for Year 2 renewal."

**Scenario 4: Reputation Risk**
- **Symptom**: Funder embroiled in scandal (corruption, unethical practices)
- **Response**: Consult legal counsel, consider returning grant if association threatens Foundation's reputation (rare, but protecting mission comes first)

## Grant Management Software & Tools

### Software Options

**Year 1-2** (Budget <$100K grants revenue):
- **Spreadsheets** (Excel, Google Sheets): Free, simple, sufficient for 5-10 grants
- **Shared Drives** (Google Drive, Dropbox): Store proposals, reports, correspondence

**Year 3-5** (Budget $500K-$2M grants revenue):
- **Grant Management Software**: Dedicated tools for prospecting, tracking, reporting

**Top Platforms**:

**1. Foundant GLM (Grant Lifecycle Manager)**:
- **Cost**: $3,000-$6,000/year (nonprofit pricing)
- **Features**: Funder database, proposal tracking, reporting calendar, document library
- **Best For**: Small-to-midsize nonprofits (5-50 grants/year)

**2. Submittable**:
- **Cost**: $5,000-$12,000/year
- **Features**: Application portal (if Foundation offers grants), funder portal, reviewer workflows
- **Best For**: Foundations that GIVE grants (not just receive)â€”Mark Egly Foundation may offer microgrants to patients (travel stipends, etc.)

**3. Instrumentl**:
- **Cost**: $1,500-$3,000/year
- **Features**: AI-powered funder matching, grant prospecting, deadline tracking, saved searches
- **Best For**: Grant prospecting (finding new funders)â€”pairs well with Foundant for full lifecycle

**4. CRM Integration (Salesforce, Bloomerang)**:
- **Cost**: Included if already using CRM for donor management
- **Features**: Track grants as "opportunities," link to funder contacts, report on pipeline
- **Best For**: Organizations with existing CRM (avoid duplicate systems)
