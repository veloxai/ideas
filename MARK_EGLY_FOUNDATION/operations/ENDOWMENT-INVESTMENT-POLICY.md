## Endowment Philosophy & Purpose

### What is an Endowment?

**Definition**: Permanent fund invested to generate incomeâ€”Foundation spends only investment returns (typically 4-5% annually), preserving principal in perpetuity.

**Purpose**:
- **Financial Stability**: Reliable income stream during economic downturns, funding gaps
- **Mission Longevity**: Ensure Foundation serves AATD patients for generations
- **Strategic Flexibility**: Discretionary funds for emerging opportunities (new treatments, research breakthroughs)
- **Donor Legacy**: Honor major donors who want lasting impact

**Endowment vs. Operating Reserves**:
- **Operating Reserves** (3-6 months expenses): Short-term safety net, liquid investments (money market, short-term bonds)
- **Endowment** (permanent): Long-term growth, diversified investments (stocks, bonds, alternatives)

---

### When to Establish Endowment

**Foundation Timeline**:
- **Year 1-3**: Focus on building operating budget, programs, donor baseâ€”no endowment yet (need cash flow for startup)
- **Year 4-5**: Once operating budget stable ($2M-$5M), begin endowment with:
  - **Major gift** ($500K-$1M founding gift from lead donor)
  - **Planned giving** (bequests, life insurance policies)
  - **Capital campaign** (multi-year effort to raise $5M-$10M endowment)

**Critical Threshold**: Don't launch endowment until Foundation has:
- âœ… 3-6 months operating reserves (financial stability first)
- âœ… Strong annual fundraising ($1M-$2M/yearâ€”proves sustainability)
- âœ… Board commitment to long-term stewardship (endowments are permanentâ€”Board must embrace)

---

### Types of Endowments

**1. Unrestricted Endowment** (Best for Flexibility):
- Board determines how income spent (general operations, programs, strategic initiatives)
- Foundation can respond to changing needs (e.g., new AATD therapy approved, need patient access program)

**2. Restricted Endowment** (Honors Donor Intent):
- Donor specifies purpose: "Income supports AATD research grants in perpetuity"
- Foundation must comply with restriction (legal obligation under donor agreement)
- Examples: Research endowment, patient assistance endowment, education endowment

**3. Quasi-Endowment** (Board-Designated):
- Board sets aside operating funds as "endowment" but retains right to invade principal if needed
- Treated like endowment for investment purposes but legally unrestricted
- Useful for growing endowment while maintaining flexibility

**Foundation Strategy**: Start with unrestricted or quasi-endowment (Year 4-5), add restricted endowments as major donors emerge (Year 6+).

## Investment Objectives & Goals

### Primary Objectives

**Objective 1: Preserve Purchasing Power** (Inflation Protection)
- Endowment must grow faster than inflation (3% historical average)
- If endowment returns 3% and Foundation spends 5%, endowment shrinks over time (intergenerational inequityâ€”future generations get less)
- **Target**: Returns > Spending Rate + Inflation + Investment Costs

**Math**:
- Spending rate: 5%
- Inflation: 3%
- Investment costs: 0.5-1%
- **Required return**: 5% + 3% + 1% = **9%/year** (before fees)
- **Realistic return**: 6-8%/year (long-term stock/bond portfolio)â€”means occasional principal invasion during market downturns (acceptable if temporary)

---

**Objective 2: Generate Stable Income** (Mission Support)
- Endowment distributions support programsâ€”must be predictable (can't triple one year, drop 50% next year)
- **Smoothing mechanism**: Spending policy averages market value over 3-5 years (see spending policy section)

**Objective 3: Grow for Future Needs** (Long-Term Growth)
- AATD research advancing (gene therapy, new treatments)â€”Foundation needs resources to scale
- Endowment should grow in real terms (after inflation, spending) by 1-2%/year

## Spending Policy & Distribution Rate

### Spending Rate Determination

**Industry Standard**: **4-5%** of endowment market value annually

**Foundation Policy**: **5%** (slightly aggressive but justified for young nonprofit with mission urgency)

**Why 5%**:
- âœ… **Mission impact**: Serves more AATD patients today (vs. hoarding for future)
- âœ… **Donor appeal**: Donors see funds used, not sitting idle
- âœ… **Feasible**: 7-8% expected returns - 5% spending = 2-3% real growth (after inflation)

**Why NOT 6-7%** (too high):
- âŒ Endowment shrinks over time (intergenerational inequityâ€”robbing future patients)
- âŒ Market downturns force principal invasion (permanent loss)
- âŒ Donor concern: "Are they raiding endowment?"

### Spending Policy Formula

**3-Year Rolling Average** (smoothing mechanism):

**Formula**:
```
Annual Distribution = 5% Ã— Average Endowment Market Value (prior 12 quarters)
```

**Example**:

| **Quarter** | **Endowment Value** |
| ----------- | ------------------- |
| Q1 2023     | $9,500,000          |
| Q2 2023     | $9,800,000          |
| Q3 2023     | $10,000,000         |
| Q4 2023     | $10,200,000         |
| Q1 2024     | $10,500,000         |
| Q2 2024     | $10,300,000         |
| Q3 2024     | $10,600,000         |
| Q4 2024     | $10,800,000         |
| Q1 2025     | $11,000,000         |
| Q2 2025     | $11,200,000         |
| Q3 2025     | $11,500,000         |
| Q4 2025     | $11,800,000         |
| **Average** | **$10,600,000**     |

**2026 Distribution**: 5% Ã— $10,600,000 = **$530,000**

---

**Benefits of Rolling Average**:
- âœ… **Stability**: Distribution changes gradually (even if market volatile)
- âœ… **Predictability**: Foundation can budget year ahead (knows approximate distribution)
- âœ… **Discipline**: Prevents over-spending in bull markets, under-spending in bear markets

**Alternative Methods** (less common):
- **Prior-year value**: 5% of Dec 31, 2025 value ($11.8M) = $590K (more volatile)
- **Fixed dollar amount**: $500K/year (doesn't grow with endowmentâ€”erodes with inflation)

---

### Distribution Timing

**Quarterly Distributions**: $530,000 Ã· 4 = **$132,500/quarter**

**Transfer Date**: 15th of January, April, July, October (aligns with operating budget cash flow)

**Process**:
1. Investment Manager calculates endowment value (prior 12 quarters average)
2. Treasurer approves distribution amount (confirms formula applied correctly)
3. Investment Manager liquidates investments (sell stocks/bonds to raise cash)
4. Wire transfer to Foundation operating account
5. Finance Director records revenue: "Endowment distributionâ€”unrestricted"

---

### Spending Flexibility

**Floor & Ceiling** (guardrails to prevent extreme distributions):

**Floor**: Minimum distribution = **3%** of current market value
- Protects mission during prolonged bear markets (if rolling average lags reality, Foundation can spend more)

**Ceiling**: Maximum distribution = **7%** of current market value
- Prevents over-spending during bull markets (if rolling average lags reality, cap spending to preserve principal)

**Example**:
- Rolling average: $10.6M â†’ 5% = $530K
- Current value: $8.0M (market crash)
- Floor: 3% Ã— $8.0M = $240K â†’ Foundation spends $240K (not $530Kâ€”would deplete endowment)

## Rebalancing Procedures

### Why Rebalance?

**Drift**: Over time, stocks outperform bonds â†’ allocation shifts from 55/30/15 to 65/25/10 (higher risk than intended).

**Rebalancing**: Sell outperformers (stocks), buy underperformers (bonds) â†’ restore target allocation.

**Benefits**:
- âœ… Maintains risk profile (prevents portfolio becoming too aggressive or conservative)
- âœ… Discipline: "Sell high, buy low" (counterintuitive but effective)
- âœ… Tax efficiency: Nonprofits don't pay capital gains tax (rebalancing is free)

---

### Rebalancing Triggers

**Calendar-Based** (Simplest):
- **Quarterly rebalancing**: Every January, April, July, October
- **Pros**: Simple, predictable
- **Cons**: May rebalance unnecessarily (if allocation still within range)

**Threshold-Based** (Foundation's Policy):
- **Rebalance when asset class drifts >5% from target**
- **Example**: Target 55% stocks, current 61% â†’ rebalance (6% drift)
- **Pros**: Reduces transaction costs, only rebalances when needed
- **Cons**: Requires monitoring

**Hybrid** (Best Practice):
- Review quarterly, rebalance if drift >5% OR at least annually (even if <5% drift)

---

### Rebalancing Process

**Step 1: Calculate Current Allocation**
- Investment Manager provides portfolio valuation: Stocks 61%, Bonds 26%, Alternatives 13%

**Step 2: Compare to Target**
- Target: 55% stocks, 30% bonds, 15% alternatives
- Drift: Stocks +6%, Bonds -4%, Alternatives -2%

**Step 3: Determine Trades**
- Sell: 6% of portfolio in stocks ($636,000 if portfolio = $10.6M)
- Buy: 4% bonds ($424,000), 2% alternatives ($212,000)

**Step 4: Execute**
- Investment Manager executes trades (same day, minimize market timing risk)

**Step 5: Confirm**
- Treasurer reviews post-rebalancing allocation: 55% stocks, 30% bonds, 15% alternatives âœ…

**Step 6: Document**
- Minutes of Investment Committee meeting: "Approved rebalancing on [Date], restoring target allocation"

## ESG & Impact Investing Considerations

### Environmental, Social, Governance (ESG) Integration

**Foundation's ESG Policy**: **Integrate ESG factors into investment decisions** (without sacrificing returns).

**Rationale**:
- âœ… **Mission alignment**: Foundation serves patientsâ€”investments shouldn't harm health (tobacco, pollution)
- âœ… **Risk management**: ESG leaders have lower regulatory, reputational, operational risks
- âœ… **Donor expectations**: Major donors (especially younger donors) expect responsible investing

---

### ESG Implementation Approaches

**Level 1: Negative Screening** (Exclusions)
- **Exclude industries**: Tobacco, firearms, fossil fuels, private prisons
- **Impact**: Reduces portfolio by ~5-10% (tobacco = 1% of S&P 500, fossil fuels = 4%)
- **Performance impact**: Minimal (tobacco underperformed S&P 500 past 20 years due to litigation, declining use)

**Level 2: Positive Screening** (Best-in-Class)
- **Overweight ESG leaders**: Companies with strong labor practices, environmental records, Board diversity
- **Tools**: MSCI ESG Ratings, Sustainalytics, CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project)
- **Example**: Invest in Microsoft (ESG leader) over ExxonMobil (ESG laggard)

**Level 3: Impact Investing** (Mission-Related Investments)
- **Direct investments in AATD-related companies**: Grifols (AATD therapy manufacturer), biotech startups
- **Risks**: Concentrated risk (single-industry), liquidity risk (private companies), conflicts (if Foundation advocates against company)
- **Allocation**: Limit to 5% of endowment (diversification protection)

**Foundation's Approach** (Year 4-7):
- **Start with Level 1** (negative screeningâ€”simple, low-cost)
- **Progress to Level 2** (Year 8+, when endowment >$10Mâ€”justify research costs)
- **Consider Level 3** (Year 13+, when endowment >$50Mâ€”can absorb risk)

### Underwater Endowments

**Definition**: Restricted endowment market value falls below original gift amount (donor gave $1M, now worth $900K).

**Cause**: Market downturn (2008, 2020 crashes), over-spending (Foundation spent 7-8% annually)

**UPMIFA Rules** (Uniform Prudent Management of Institutional Funds Act):
- **Old rule** (pre-2008): Could NOT spend from underwater endowments (legally prohibitedâ€”principal sacrosanct)
- **New rule** (post-2008): CAN spend prudently from underwater endowments (considering duration, expected return, Foundation needs)

**Foundation Policy**:
- If endowment underwater <10% (worth $950K on $1M gift), continue spending per policy (3-year rolling average smooths this)
- If underwater >10%, reduce spending to 3-4% (preserve principal, allow recovery)
- Communicate transparently with donor (if living): "Market downturn temporarily reduced fund, we're managing prudently, expect recovery in X years"

## Conflict of Interest Management

### Disclosure Requirements

**Annual Disclosure** (all Investment Committee members):
- List personal investments, family business interests, Board seats at other organizations
- Identify potential conflicts (e.g., Committee member owns stock in company Foundation might invest in)

**Ongoing Disclosure**: If conflict arises mid-year (Committee member joins investment firm), disclose immediately.

---

### Recusal Procedures

**If Conflict Identified**:
1. Member discloses conflict ("I own shares in XYZ fund we're considering")
2. Chair determines if material (>$10K personal investment, or >1% of Foundation portfolioâ€”material)
3. If material, member recuses (leaves room during vote, does not participate in discussion)
4. Minutes document recusal ("John Smith recused due to personal holdings in XYZ fund")
5. Remaining members vote (quorum = 50% of non-conflicted members)

### Transparency vs. Confidentiality

**Public Disclosure** (Form 990, annual report):

**Confidential** (Investment Committee, Board only):
- Specific fund holdings (disclosing holdings creates front-running riskâ€”traders exploit information)
- Investment manager identity (in some casesâ€”though often public)

**Rationale**: Balance transparency (accountability to donors, public) with prudence (protecting investment strategy from exploitation).
