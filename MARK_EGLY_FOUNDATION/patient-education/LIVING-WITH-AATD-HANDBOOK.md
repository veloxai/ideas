## Your First 30 Days After Diagnosis

### Week 1: Process & Learn

**It's normal to feel**:

- Overwhelmed
- Scared
- Confused
- Angry
- Relieved (finally have answers)

**What to do**:

- ‚úÖ Take time to process the diagnosis
- ‚úÖ Read "What is AATD?" guide
- ‚úÖ Make list of questions for your doctor
- ‚úÖ Tell a trusted friend or family member
- ‚úÖ Join an AATD support group (online or in-person)

**Don't**:

- ‚ùå Google everything at once (information overload)
- ‚ùå Make major life decisions immediately
- ‚ùå Isolate yourself

---

### Week 2: Build Your Team

**Schedule appointments**:

1. **Pulmonologist** (lung specialist)
   - Preferably AATD-experienced (find AATD Aware Certified provider)
   - Get baseline tests: Spirometry, CT scan, walking test
2. **Primary care physician**
   - Update them on diagnosis
   - Discuss vaccinations
3. **Genetic counselor** (optional but recommended)
   - Understand inheritance
   - Discuss family testing

**Contact Mark Egly Foundation**:

- Patient navigation services
- Financial assistance information
- Support group connections

---

### Week 3: Get Baseline Data

**Tests you need**:

- **Spirometry**: Measures lung function (FEV1, FVC)
- **Chest CT scan**: Images lung damage
- **6-minute walk test**: Assesses exercise capacity
- **Liver function tests**: Blood tests (ALT, AST, bilirubin)
- **Complete blood count (CBC)**
- **Oxygen saturation test**

**Why baseline matters**:

- Establishes starting point
- Tracks disease progression over time
- Helps determine if/when to start treatment

---

### Week 4: Create Your Plan

**Discuss with your doctor**:

1. **Treatment options**
   - Am I candidate for augmentation therapy?
   - What medications do I need?
   - Do I need oxygen?
2. **Lifestyle modifications**
   - Smoking cessation plan (if applicable)
   - Exercise program
   - Nutrition guidance
3. **Monitoring schedule**
   - How often will I be tested?
   - What symptoms should I watch for?
4. **Family testing**
   - Who should be tested?
   - How to talk to family about it

**Create action plan**:

- Write down treatment plan
- Schedule follow-up appointments
- Set up medication reminders
- Join support group

## Building Your Healthcare Team

### Your AATD Dream Team

**1. Pulmonologist (Lung Specialist)**

- **Your quarterback**: Manages overall AATD care
- **Look for**: AATD Aware Certified or experience treating AATD patients
- **Frequency**: Every 3-6 months (more often if symptomatic)

**2. Primary Care Physician (PCP)**

- **Your general health manager**: Annual checkups, vaccinations, other health issues
- **Frequency**: Annual minimum

**3. Hepatologist (Liver Specialist)**

- **When needed**: If you have elevated liver enzymes or cirrhosis
- **Frequency**: Every 6-12 months if liver disease present

**4. Respiratory Therapist**

- **Helps with**: Breathing techniques, oxygen therapy, pulmonary rehab
- **Frequency**: As needed

**5. Genetic Counselor**

- **Helps with**: Understanding inheritance, family planning, family testing
- **Frequency**: One-time or as needed

**6. Mental Health Professional**

- **Helps with**: Coping with diagnosis, anxiety, depression
- **Frequency**: As needed

**7. Registered Dietitian**

- **Helps with**: Nutrition for lung health, weight management
- **Frequency**: Initial consultation, then as needed

**8. Social Worker or Patient Navigator**

- **Helps with**: Insurance, financial assistance, resources
- **Mark Egly Foundation provides this FREE**

---

### Questions to Ask Your Doctor

**At diagnosis**:

- What is my AAT level and genotype?
- How damaged are my lungs/liver?
- What is my prognosis?
- What treatment do you recommend?
- How often should I be monitored?

**At follow-ups**:

- Has my lung function changed?
- Do I need to adjust treatment?
- Are there new treatments or clinical trials?
- What can I do to slow progression?

**About augmentation therapy**:

- Am I a candidate?
- How is it administered?
- What are side effects?
- How much will it cost?
- How do I get insurance approval?

## Daily Health Management

### Morning Routine

**Medications & Treatments**:

- ‚úÖ Take prescribed inhalers/medications
- ‚úÖ Check oxygen saturation (if you have pulse oximeter)
- ‚úÖ Do breathing exercises (5-10 minutes)

**Self-Assessment**:

- How's my breathing today?
- Any new symptoms?
- Energy level?

**Planning**:

- Schedule activities during high-energy times
- Pace yourself (don't overdo it)

---

### Throughout the Day

**Energy Conservation**:

- Sit when you can (e.g., sit while getting dressed, cooking)
- Use assistive devices (shower chair, grabber tools)
- Take breaks before you get tired
- Delegate heavy tasks

**Breathing Techniques**:

- **Pursed-lip breathing**: Breathe in through nose, exhale slowly through pursed lips
- **Diaphragmatic breathing**: Use belly, not chest
- Use during shortness of breath episodes

**Hydration**:

- Drink 6-8 glasses of water daily
- Keeps mucus thin and easier to cough up

**Avoid Triggers**:

- Smoke (cigarettes, vaping, secondhand smoke)
- Air pollution (stay inside on high pollution days)
- Strong scents (perfumes, cleaners, paint)
- Dust and mold

---

### Evening Routine

**Medications**:

- Take evening medications as prescribed
- Prepare tomorrow's meds

**Symptom Tracking**:

- Log any symptoms in journal or app
- Note activity level and how you felt

**Relaxation**:

- Stress management (meditation, reading, gentle stretching)
- Good sleep hygiene (cool room, dark, quiet)

## Nutrition & Exercise

### Nutrition for AATD

**Goals**:

- Maintain healthy weight
- Support immune system
- Reduce inflammation
- Preserve muscle mass

**What to Eat**:

- ‚úÖ **Protein**: Lean meats, fish, eggs, beans (maintain muscle)
- ‚úÖ **Fruits & vegetables**: Antioxidants, vitamins (5+ servings/day)
- ‚úÖ **Whole grains**: Energy, fiber
- ‚úÖ **Healthy fats**: Omega-3s (fish, nuts, olive oil) reduce inflammation
- ‚úÖ **Water**: Stay hydrated

**What to Limit**:

- ‚ùå **Processed foods**: High sodium (can cause fluid retention)
- ‚ùå **Alcohol**: Hard on liver (especially if liver disease present)
- ‚ùå **Sugary foods**: Can cause energy crashes

---

### Weight Management

**If underweight** (common with advanced lung disease):

- Eat smaller, frequent meals (5-6 per day)
- Add calories with healthy fats (nut butters, avocado, olive oil)
- Protein shakes or supplements
- Talk to dietitian about appetite stimulants

**If overweight**:

- Extra weight makes breathing harder
- Work with dietitian on gradual weight loss
- Focus on nutrient-dense, lower-calorie foods
- Gentle exercise as tolerated

---

### Exercise & Activity

**Why exercise matters**:

- ‚úÖ Improves lung function
- ‚úÖ Strengthens muscles (including breathing muscles)
- ‚úÖ Boosts energy and mood
- ‚úÖ Slows disease progression

**Types of exercise**:

**1. Aerobic (Cardio)**:

- Walking, swimming, cycling, dancing
- Start with 5-10 minutes, gradually increase
- Goal: 20-30 minutes, 3-5 times per week
- Use pursed-lip breathing during activity

**2. Strength Training**:

- Light weights or resistance bands
- Focus on arms, legs, core
- 2-3 times per week
- Helps with daily activities

**3. Flexibility**:

- Stretching, yoga, tai chi
- Improves posture and breathing
- Daily or several times per week

**4. Breathing Exercises**:

- Pursed-lip breathing
- Diaphragmatic breathing
- Incentive spirometry
- 5-10 minutes, 2-3 times daily

---

### Pulmonary Rehabilitation

**What it is**:

- Supervised exercise and education program
- Typically 6-12 weeks, 2-3 sessions per week
- Led by respiratory therapists

**What you'll do**:

- Monitored exercise (treadmill, bike, weights)
- Breathing techniques
- Energy conservation strategies
- Education on COPD/AATD management
- Nutritional counseling
- Emotional support

**Why it helps**:

- Improves exercise tolerance
- Reduces shortness of breath
- Increases quality of life
- Builds confidence

**How to access**:

- Ask your pulmonologist for referral
- Usually covered by insurance
- Some programs offer virtual/home options

## Managing Symptoms

### Shortness of Breath

**Immediate relief**:

1. Stop activity and rest
2. Use pursed-lip breathing
3. Find comfortable position:
   - Sit and lean forward, arms on knees
   - Stand and lean against wall
   - Tripod position
4. Use rescue inhaler if prescribed
5. Stay calm (anxiety makes it worse)

**When to call doctor**:

- Shortness of breath is new or worsening
- Doesn't improve with rest or inhaler
- Accompanied by chest pain or confusion

**When to call 911**:

- Severe shortness of breath
- Blue lips or fingernails
- Chest pain
- Confusion or fainting

---

### Chronic Cough

**Management**:

- Stay hydrated (thins mucus)
- Use humidifier (adds moisture to air)
- Avoid irritants (smoke, dust, strong scents)
- Try honey or cough drops (soothing)
- Elevate head while sleeping

**When to call doctor**:

- Coughing up blood
- Cough with fever
- Yellow/green mucus (possible infection)
- Cough keeping you awake

---

### Fatigue

**Causes**:

- Low oxygen levels
- Poor sleep
- Depression/anxiety
- Deconditioning (lack of activity)

**Management**:

- Pace activities (don't do everything at once)
- Prioritize (do important tasks first)
- Take short rest breaks
- Exercise regularly (builds stamina)
- Get good sleep (7-9 hours)
- Treat underlying causes (oxygen, depression treatment)

---

### Frequent Infections

**Prevention**:

- ‚úÖ Get vaccinated (flu, pneumonia, COVID-19)
- ‚úÖ Wash hands frequently
- ‚úÖ Avoid sick people
- ‚úÖ Don't touch face
- ‚úÖ Stay active and eat well (immune support)

**When to call doctor**:

- Fever (>100.4¬∞F)
- Increased cough or mucus
- Change in mucus color (green, yellow, blood)
- Worsening shortness of breath
- Chest pain

**Early treatment matters**: Lung infections can cause rapid decline in AATD

## Treatment Schedules

### Augmentation Therapy

**What it is**: Weekly IV infusions of AAT protein

**Typical schedule**:

- Once weekly (usually same day/time each week)
- 1-2 hours per infusion
- At infusion center, doctor's office, or home

**Tips for success**:

- **Schedule consistency**: Same day/time helps you remember
- **Hydration**: Drink water before and during infusion
- **Bring entertainment**: Book, tablet, music
- **Track side effects**: Note any headache, fatigue, allergic reactions
- **Rotate IV sites**: Prevents vein damage

**Don't skip doses**: Consistent levels are key to effectiveness

---

### Daily Medications

**Typical AATD medication regimen**:

**Inhalers**:

- **Bronchodilators**: Open airways (short-acting for rescue, long-acting for maintenance)
- **Inhaled corticosteroids**: Reduce inflammation
- **How to remember**: Set phone alarms, use pill organizer

**Other Medications**:

- **Antibiotics**: Keep on hand for infections (call doctor at first sign)
- **Prednisone**: Steroid for exacerbations (use only as prescribed)
- **Oxygen**: As prescribed (often during activity, sleep, or 24/7)

**Medication tracker**:

- Use app (Medisafe, MyMedSchedule) or paper chart
- Check off as you take them
- Note any missed doses

---

### Monitoring Schedule

**Every 3-6 months** (routine checkup):

- Spirometry (lung function test)
- Oxygen saturation
- 6-minute walk test
- Symptom review

**Annually**:

- Chest CT scan
- Liver function tests (if liver disease)
- Comprehensive physical exam
- Vaccinations (flu, etc.)

**As needed**:

- When symptoms worsen
- Before starting new treatment
- During/after respiratory infection

## Emotional & Mental Health

### It's Okay to Not Be Okay

**Living with AATD is hard**:

- Chronic illness is emotionally draining
- Uncertainty about future
- Lifestyle changes and limitations
- Financial stress
- Family worry

**Common emotions**:

- Anxiety
- Depression
- Anger or frustration
- Grief (for life before AATD)
- Fear

**You're not alone**: Most AATD patients experience these feelings

---

### Coping Strategies

**1. Connect with others**:

- Join AATD support group (in-person or online)
- Talk to family and friends
- Consider therapy or counseling

**2. Stay informed (but not obsessed)**:

- Learn about AATD, but don't spiral into worst-case scenarios
- Ask doctor questions
- Focus on what you can control

**3. Practice self-care**:

- Do things you enjoy
- Maintain hobbies (adapt as needed)
- Relaxation techniques (meditation, deep breathing, yoga)

**4. Set realistic goals**:

- Celebrate small wins
- Adjust expectations (new normal)
- Focus on what you CAN do, not what you can't

**5. Seek professional help if**:

- Depression lasting >2 weeks
- Thoughts of self-harm
- Panic attacks
- Anxiety interfering with daily life

**Therapy helps**: Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) effective for chronic illness adjustment

---

### Talking to Loved Ones

**Be honest**:

- Explain AATD and what it means
- Share how you're feeling
- Let them know how they can help

**What to say**:

- "I have a genetic condition called AATD that affects my lungs."
- "Some days I'll feel good, other days I'll struggle."
- "I need you to be patient when I can't keep up."
- "It helps when you [specific thing they do]."

**What you might need**:

- Someone to listen (not fix)
- Help with errands or chores
- Companionship at appointments
- Reminders to take meds or exercise

## Work & Career

### Continuing to Work

**Good news**: Many people with AATD work full-time for years

**Tips**:

- **Disclose selectively**: Not required to tell employer unless you need accommodations
- **Request accommodations**: ADA protects you (flexible schedule, work from home, ergonomic setup, avoid irritants)
- **Pace yourself**: Use breaks, don't overexert
- **Know your limits**: It's okay to reduce hours or change roles

---

### Career Considerations

**Jobs to avoid** (or modify):

- Heavy physical labor
- Exposure to dust, fumes, chemicals
- Extreme temperatures
- High-stress (can trigger symptoms)

**Jobs well-suited for AATD**:

- Desk work, office jobs
- Remote/work-from-home
- Creative fields
- Consulting or freelance (flexible schedule)

---

### Disability Benefits

**When to consider**:

- Can't work full-time due to symptoms
- Job limitations significantly impact income

**Types**:

- **Short-term disability**: Through employer or private insurance (temporary)
- **Long-term disability**: Private insurance (typically 60% of salary)
- **Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI)**: Federal program (must meet criteria)

**How to apply**:

- **SSDI**: Apply online at ssa.gov or call 1-800-772-1213
- **Private disability**: Contact your insurance company

**Documentation needed**:

- Medical records (spirometry, CT scans, doctor notes)
- Statement from doctor about limitations
- Work history

**Mark Egly Foundation can help**: Patient navigators assist with disability applications

## Travel Tips

### Can I Travel with AATD?

**Yes!** With planning:

- Most AATD patients travel successfully
- Key is preparation

---

### Planning Your Trip

**1. Talk to your doctor**:

- Is it safe for me to travel?
- Do I need supplemental oxygen during flight?
- Prescription for antibiotics "just in case"?

**2. Bring extra medications**:

- Pack double what you need (in case of delays)
- Carry-on bag (don't check)
- Original prescription bottles (for TSA/customs)

**3. Arrange oxygen (if needed)**:

- **Flights**: Call airline 48-72 hours ahead; bring doctor's prescription
- **Destination**: Rent oxygen concentrator or arrange delivery
- **Car travel**: Portable oxygen concentrator (POC)

**4. Travel insurance**:

- Consider policy that covers medical emergencies
- Read fine print (pre-existing conditions may be excluded)

**5. Research healthcare at destination**:

- Know where nearest hospital is
- Bring health insurance card
- International travel: Check if insurance covers abroad

---

### Packing List

**Medications & Medical**:

- All prescription medications (carry-on)
- Copies of prescriptions
- Rescue inhaler
- Pulse oximeter
- Doctor's contact information
- Medical summary (diagnosis, medications, allergies)

**Comfort Items**:

- Hand sanitizer, disinfectant wipes
- Face mask (protect from germs, irritants)
- Neck pillow (for flights)
- Snacks and water bottle

---

### During Travel

**Flights**:

- Request aisle seat (easier to get up, access restroom)
- Walk around every 1-2 hours (prevent blood clots)
- Stay hydrated (cabin air is dry)
- Use nasal saline spray (combat dry air)

**General Tips**:

- Pace yourself (don't overpack itinerary)
- Rest when tired (it's okay to skip activities)
- Avoid irritants (smoke, pollution, allergens)
- Wash hands frequently

## Financial Navigation

### The Cost of AATD

**Expensive treatments**:

- Augmentation therapy: $100K-$200K per year
- Lung transplant: $500K-$1M+
- Ongoing medications, tests, doctor visits

**Good news**: You shouldn't have to pay full cost

---

### Insurance Coverage

**Most insurance covers AATD treatment**:

- Commercial/employer insurance
- Medicare (age 65+ or disabled)
- Medicaid (income-based)

**What's typically covered**:

- Augmentation therapy (if FEV1 <80% and clinically indicated)
- Medications and inhalers
- Doctor visits and tests
- Pulmonary rehabilitation
- Oxygen therapy

**Prior authorization**:

- Insurance may require approval before expensive treatments
- Your doctor submits documentation
- Can take 2-4 weeks

**If denied**:

- Appeal! Many denials are overturned
- Get help from patient navigator or social worker

---

### Financial Assistance Programs

**1. Pharmaceutical Patient Assistance Programs (PAPs)**:

- Free or reduced-cost augmentation therapy for uninsured/underinsured
- Each manufacturer has own program:
  - Grifols (Prolastin): AlphaID
  - CSL Behring (Zemaira)
  - Takeda (Glassia)
- Apply through your specialty pharmacy or doctor

**2. Co-pay Assistance**:

- Help with out-of-pocket costs (deductibles, co-pays)
- Offered by manufacturers
- Income limits may apply

**3. Mark Egly Foundation Grants**:

- Financial assistance for testing, treatment, travel to specialists
- See "Grants OUT Program" documents
- Apply at MarkEglyFoundation.org/grants

**4. Other Nonprofits**:

- AlphaNet: Disease management program (free)
- HealthWell Foundation: Co-pay assistance
- Patient Advocate Foundation: Co-pay relief

---

### Tracking Expenses

**Keep records**:

- Medical expenses are tax-deductible (if >7.5% of income)
- Save receipts: Medications, doctor visits, travel to appointments, medical equipment

**Create system**:

- Folder or binder for medical bills
- Spreadsheet to track expenses
- Apps: Medici, My Medical (receipt scanning)

## Family & Relationships

### Telling Your Family

**Who should know**:

- Immediate family (parents, siblings, children, partner)
- Extended family (if close relationship or if they should get tested)

**How to tell them**:

- **In person**: Best for immediate family
- **Phone or video call**: For distant family
- **Written (email/letter)**: If you prefer to organize thoughts

**What to say**:

- "I've been diagnosed with a genetic condition called Alpha-1."
- "It affects my lungs [and liver], but it's manageable."
- "Because it's genetic, you may want to get tested."
- "Here's information to share with your doctor."

---

### Family Testing

**Who should be tested**:

- **Siblings**: Same parents = same risk
- **Children**: 50-100% chance of being carriers
- **Parents**: Likely carriers (if you have ZZ)
- **Extended family**: Aunts, uncles, cousins (especially if family history of lung/liver disease)

**How to encourage testing**:

- Share educational materials (Mark Egly Foundation brochures)
- Offer to help them talk to their doctor
- Explain it's simple blood test
- Emphasize early detection benefits

**If family resists**:

- Don't push too hard
- Some people aren't ready
- Leave door open: "Let me know if you change your mind."

---

### Impact on Relationships

**Partners**:

- Chronic illness adds stress to relationships
- Communicate openly about needs and fears
- Ask for help (don't be martyr)
- Maintain intimacy (adapt as needed)
- Consider couples counseling if struggling

**Children**:

- Age-appropriate honesty
- Young kids: "Mommy/Daddy has a condition that makes it harder to breathe sometimes."
- Teens: More detailed explanation, discuss genetic aspects
- Reassure them you're taking care of yourself

**Friends**:

- True friends will stick around
- Let them know how they can help
- It's okay if some relationships fade (focus energy on supportive people)
