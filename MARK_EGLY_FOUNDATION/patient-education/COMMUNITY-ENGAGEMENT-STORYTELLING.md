## Overview

### What is Patient Storytelling?

**Patient storytelling** is the strategic collection, production, and sharing of authentic AATD patient narratives to:

- ‚úÖ **Raise Awareness**: Humanize AATD (faces, names, families‚Äînot just medical statistics)
- ‚úÖ **Reduce Isolation**: Show patients "You're not alone" (see others thriving despite diagnosis)
- ‚úÖ **Educate**: Teach through experience (symptoms, diagnosis journey, treatment, coping strategies)
- ‚úÖ **Inspire Action**: Motivate advocacy (legislators moved by stories, not data)
- ‚úÖ **Build Community**: Create connections (patients recognize themselves in others' stories)
- ‚úÖ **Attract Donors**: Move hearts, open wallets (donors give to people, not causes)

**Stories are the Foundation's currency** ‚Üí more powerful than any statistic, brochure, or website

## Why Storytelling Matters

### The Science of Storytelling

**Research shows stories are more effective than facts**:

- **23x more memorable** than facts alone (brain remembers narratives, not statistics)
- **4x more persuasive** for advocacy (legislators vote based on constituent stories, not policy briefs)
- **2x more donations** (donors give more when shown individual story vs. aggregate data)
- **Empathy activation**: Stories trigger mirror neurons (reader/viewer feels patient's emotions)

**Source**: Stanford Social Innovation Review, Johns Hopkins Center for Communication Programs

---

### AATD Storytelling Imperative

**Why AATD needs stories especially**:

- **Unknown disease** (99% of Americans have never heard of AATD‚Äîstories make it real)
- **Misunderstood** ("Just another lung disease" ‚Üí No, genetic, rare, complex)
- **Invisible illness** (many patients look healthy‚Äîstories reveal hidden struggles)
- **Diagnostic delay crisis** (7-8 years average‚Äîstories show consequences, motivate change)
- **Policy inaction** (no federal newborn screening‚Äîstories move legislators to act)

## Story Types & Purposes

### 1. Diagnosis Stories (30% of Story Bank)

**Purpose**: Educate about diagnostic delay, symptoms, misdiagnosis

**Structure**:

- **Before Diagnosis**: Years of symptoms (shortness of breath, misdiagnosed as asthma)
- **Turning Point**: How finally diagnosed (new doctor, self-advocacy, genetic testing)
- **After Diagnosis**: Relief (finally know what's wrong), fear (what now?), hope (there's treatment)

**Key Messages**:

- Early diagnosis matters (treatment works best when started early)
- Advocate for yourself (if doctor dismisses symptoms, seek second opinion)
- Testing is easy (simple blood test‚Äîdon't delay)

**Examples**:

- "I Was Misdiagnosed for 10 Years‚ÄîDoctors Said It Was Asthma"
- "I Diagnosed Myself‚ÄîThen Saved My Family"
- "My Doctor Had Never Heard of AATD‚ÄîI Had to Educate Her"

---

### 2. Living Well Stories (25% of Story Bank)

**Purpose**: Inspire hope, show AATD patients can thrive

**Structure**:

- **Challenges**: Disease limitations (oxygen use, fatigue, frequent exacerbations)
- **Adaptation**: How patient adjusted (pacing, accommodations, mindset shifts)
- **Thriving**: Full life despite AATD (career, family, hobbies, advocacy)

**Key Messages**:

- AATD is not a death sentence (many patients live long, fulfilling lives)
- Treatment works (augmentation therapy slows disease, improves quality of life)
- Community helps (support groups, Alpha1Life.com, Foundation resources)

**Examples**:

- "I Climbed a Mountain with AATD (and Oxygen)"
- "I Run a Business, Raise Three Kids, and Manage AATD‚ÄîHere's How"
- "I'm 72, Have AATD, and I'm Living My Best Life"

---

### 3. Advocacy Stories (15% of Story Bank)

**Purpose**: Motivate policy change, recruit advocates

**Structure**:

- **The Problem**: Policy gap (no newborn screening, insurance denials, discrimination)
- **Taking Action**: How patient advocated (testified, organized, lobbied)
- **The Win**: Change achieved (bill passed, coverage approved, awareness raised)

**Key Messages**:

- You can make a difference (legislators listen to constituents)
- Collective action works (organize, amplify voices)
- Advocacy is for everyone (don't need experience‚Äîjust passion)

**Examples**:

- "I Testified at My State Capitol‚ÄîAnd We Passed Newborn Screening"
- "I Fought My Insurance for 2 Years‚ÄîAnd Won"
- "How 100 AATD Patients Changed Federal Policy"

---

### 4. Caregiver & Family Stories (15% of Story Bank)

**Purpose**: Recognize caregivers, address family dynamics, genetic implications

**Structure**:

- **Caregiver Perspective**: Watching loved one struggle (helplessness, fear, burnout)
- **Family Impact**: Genetic testing, siblings at risk, family planning decisions
- **Coping & Hope**: How family adapted, found support, grew stronger

**Key Messages**:

- Caregivers matter (Foundation supports families, not just patients)
- Genetic testing saves lives (test siblings, children‚Äîearly diagnosis prevents harm)
- Families are stronger together (support groups, family counseling, shared advocacy)

**Examples**:

- "My Husband Has AATD‚ÄîHere's How I Support Him Without Burning Out"
- "I Tested My Kids for AATD‚ÄîBest Decision I Ever Made"
- "Our Son's AATD Brought Our Family Closer"

---

### 5. Medical Professional Stories (10% of Story Bank)

**Purpose**: Educate providers, reduce diagnostic delay, recruit champions

**Structure**:

- **Missed Diagnosis**: Doctor shares case they missed (patient suffered, learned lesson)
- **Diagnostic Success**: Doctor shares how they caught AATD early (saved patient's life)
- **Professional Commitment**: Why doctor now screens all COPD patients, advocates for awareness

**Key Messages**:

- Test for AATD (in young COPD, non-smokers, family history)
- Diagnostic delay has consequences (legal, moral, patient outcomes)
- Doctors can be champions (educate colleagues, advocate for screening)

**Examples**:

- "I Missed AATD for 5 Years‚ÄîNow I Test Everyone"
- "This Simple Test Saved My Patient's Life"
- "Why Every Pulmonologist Should Screen for AATD"

---

### 6. Loss & Memorial Stories (5% of Story Bank‚ÄîHandle Sensitively)

**Purpose**: Honor those lost, motivate urgency for research and awareness

**Structure**:

- **Life Cut Short**: Patient died from AATD (late diagnosis, no treatment, complications)
- **Family Remembers**: Loved ones share story, mission to prevent others' suffering
- **Call to Action**: Donate, advocate, test‚Äîin memory of patient

**Key Messages**:

- AATD is serious (can be fatal if untreated)
- Early diagnosis saves lives (honor the lost by preventing future deaths)
- Donate to research (find better treatments, cure)

**Examples**:

- "In Memory of My Sister‚ÄîWhy I Fight for AATD Awareness"
- "He Was Diagnosed Too Late‚ÄîLet's Make Sure No One Else Is"

## Story Collection Process

### Step 1: Identify Storytellers

**Outreach Methods**:

- **Support Groups**: Ask leaders to nominate storytellers (patients who speak up in meetings‚Äîcompelling, articulate)
- **Alpha1Life.com**: "Share Your Story" form (open submission‚Äîany patient can submit)
- **Navigator Referrals**: Patient Navigators identify great stories (during case management, ask "Would you share your story?")
- **Social Media**: Monitor Facebook, Instagram (patients already sharing‚Äîask permission to feature)
- **Events**: Conferences, awareness walks (recruit storytellers at Foundation events)

**Ideal Storytellers**:

- ‚úÖ Articulate (can explain AATD clearly, compelling speaker/writer)
- ‚úÖ Authentic (vulnerable, honest‚Äînot polished or scripted)
- ‚úÖ Diverse (represent all ages, races, genders, genotypes, disease stages‚Äînot just young, white, mildly affected)
- ‚úÖ Willing (comfortable sharing publicly‚Äîname, photo, medical details)
- ‚úÖ Available (responsive, meet deadlines, participate in promotion)

---

### Step 2: Pre-Interview & Consent

**Initial Contact**:

- Foundation staff calls patient (15-min conversation‚Äîexplain storytelling program, what's involved)
- **Set Expectations**: "We'd love to share your story on Alpha1Life.com, social media, maybe newsletters. You'd do a 60-min interview, we'd draft story, you'd review and approve before publishing. Interested?"

**Consent Process**:

- Send **Storytelling Consent Form** (patient signs‚Äîgives permission to use name, photo, story)
- **Key Clauses**:
  - Right to review (patient approves final story before publishing)
  - Photo/video release (Foundation can use images in marketing, social media, fundraising)
  - Revocation (patient can withdraw story anytime‚ÄîFoundation will remove)
  - No compensation (storytelling is volunteer‚Äîbut Foundation may cover travel to events)

**HIPAA Considerations**:

- Patient **controls** what they share (no medical details without their explicit consent)
- Foundation does **not** access medical records (patient self-reports‚Äîthey decide what's public)

---

### Step 3: Interview & Story Capture

**Interview Methods**:

- **Phone Interview** (60-90 min‚Äîmost common, convenient for patient)
- **Video Call** (Zoom‚Äîif story will be video, capture interview footage)
- **In-Person** (conferences, support groups‚Äîdeeper connection, body language)
- **Written Submission** (patient writes own story‚Äîless work for Foundation, but fewer patients willing)

**Interview Guide** (Questions to Ask):

1. **Diagnosis Journey**: "When were you diagnosed? What symptoms did you have? How long did it take?"
2. **Turning Point**: "What finally led to diagnosis? How did you feel when you got the news?"
3. **Treatment**: "Are you on augmentation therapy? How has it helped? Any challenges?"
4. **Daily Life**: "How does AATD affect your daily life? Work? Family? Hobbies?"
5. **Support**: "What resources have helped most? Support groups? Alpha1Life? Foundation?"
6. **Hope**: "What gives you hope? What would you say to newly diagnosed patients?"
7. **Advocacy**: "Are you involved in advocacy? Why is it important to you?"
8. **Call to Action**: "What do you want readers/viewers to take away from your story?"

**Recording**:

- Audio record (with permission‚Äîfor transcription)
- Take notes (key quotes, emotional moments)
- Request photos (patient provides 3-5 photos‚Äîheadshot, family, hobbies)

---

### Step 4: Story Production

**Written Stories** (800-1,200 words):

- **Draft**: Foundation writer/intern drafts story (based on interview, in patient's voice‚Äîfirst person)
- **Structure**:
  - **Hook** (1-2 paragraphs‚Äîgrab attention, establish stakes)
  - **Diagnosis Journey** (3-4 paragraphs‚Äîsymptoms, misdiagnosis, finally getting diagnosed)
  - **Treatment & Management** (2-3 paragraphs‚Äîaugmentation therapy, lifestyle adjustments, coping)
  - **Life Today** (2-3 paragraphs‚Äîwork, family, hobbies‚Äîpatient thriving)
  - **Message of Hope** (1-2 paragraphs‚Äîadvice for others, call to action)
- **Review**: Send draft to patient (patient edits, approves‚Äîusually 1-2 rounds of revisions)
- **Final Approval**: Patient signs off ("Yes, publish this")

**Video Stories** (3-5 minutes):

- **Script**: Based on written story (patient speaks to camera, tells story in their words)
- **Filming**: Professional videographer (if budget allows) or DIY (patient records on phone, Foundation edits)
- **Editing**: Add B-roll (patient at home, with family, doing activities), music, Foundation branding
- **Review**: Patient watches final video, approves before publishing

**Photo Stories** (Social Media):

- **Format**: Single photo + caption (100-200 words‚Äîpatient's story in brief)
- **Quick & Shareable**: For Instagram, Facebook (drive traffic to full story on website)

## Storytelling Ethics & Consent

### Ethical Guidelines

**1. Informed Consent Always**:

- Patient signs consent form (before any story published)
- Explain exactly how story will be used (website? social media? fundraising materials?)
- Patient can revoke consent anytime (Foundation removes story promptly)

**2. Patient Controls Narrative**:

- Patient decides what to share (medical details, photos, family names)
- Foundation never pressures (if patient uncomfortable, respect boundaries)
- Patient approves final story (review, edit, approve before publishing)

**3. No Exploitation**:

- Don't sensationalize ("Dying from AATD" clickbait)
- Don't guilt-trip ("Donate or patients die")
- Respect dignity (especially end-of-life, pediatric stories‚Äîhandle with care)

**4. Diverse Representation**:

- Seek stories from all demographics (not just young, white, affluent patients)
- Represent all disease stages (mild, severe, transplant, carriers‚Äînot just "inspiring" stories)
- Avoid tokenism (don't feature one minority patient to check box)

**5. Privacy Protection**:

- Use only patient-provided info (no medical records, no third-party info)
- Protect minors (parental consent for children, limit identifying details)
- Anonymity option (if patient wants to share story but not name, use pseudonym)

---

### Sensitive Situations

**Loss & Memorial Stories**:

- Approach family gently (express condolences first, wait appropriate time before asking)
- Ensure family wants to share (don't pressure‚Äîsome need privacy in grief)
- Honor patient's memory (family reviews, approves story)

**Pediatric Stories**:

- Parental consent required (both parents if possible)
- Child's assent (if old enough‚Äîask "Is it OK if we share your story?")
- Limit identifying info (school name, city‚Äîprotect child's privacy)

**Mental Health Struggles**:

- Handle carefully (depression, suicidal ideation‚Äîdon't glamorize, provide resources)
- Trigger warnings (if story discusses self-harm, give readers heads-up)
- Resource links (988 Suicide Hotline, crisis text line‚Äîalways include)
