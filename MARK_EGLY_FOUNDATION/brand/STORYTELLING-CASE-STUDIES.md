## Why Stories Matter

**Data tells, stories sell.**

- ‚úÖ **Emotional Connection**: Stories create empathy and move people to action

- ‚úÖ **Humanization**: Stories put faces and names to abstract problems
- ‚úÖ **Persuasion**: Stories convince donors, policymakers, and media to support our cause
- ‚úÖ **Inspiration**: Stories give hope to newly diagnosed patients

**Statistics set the stage. Stories close the deal.**

## Storytelling Framework

### The Diagnostic Journey Arc

**Every AATD story follows a similar arc**:

```
1. Life Before ‚Üí 2. First Symptoms ‚Üí 3. Medical Run-Around ‚Üí 4. Diagnosis ‚Üí 5. Treatment & Advocacy ‚Üí 6. Life Now
```

---

### Story Arc Breakdown

**1. Life Before Symptoms**

- Who was the person before AATD?
- What did they love to do? (work, hobbies, family)
- Set the stage: "This could be anyone."

**2. First Symptoms**

- When did they first notice something was wrong?
- What symptoms? (shortness of breath, coughing, fatigue)
- How did it impact daily life?

**3. The Medical Run-Around** (the crisis)

- How many doctors did they see?
- What were they told? (misdiagnoses: asthma, COPD, anxiety)
- How long did this go on?
- Emotional toll: frustration, fear, confusion
- **This is the diagnostic delay we're fighting.**

**4. The Diagnosis** (the turning point)

- How did they finally get diagnosed?
- Who ordered the right test?
- What was the reaction? (relief, fear, anger, confusion)

**5. Treatment & Advocacy**

- What treatment did they start? (augmentation therapy, lifestyle changes)
- How has it helped?
- How are they now advocating? (support groups, speaking out, policy work)

**6. Life Now**

- How is life different post-diagnosis?
- What's their message to others?
- What gives them hope?

---

### Story Elements That Work

**Specific Details**: Don't say "She felt bad." Say "She couldn't walk from her car to the grocery store without stopping to catch her breath."

**Dialogue**: Include direct quotes. "The doctor said, 'It's just anxiety. You need to relax.'"

**Emotion**: Show the frustration, fear, relief, hope.

**Relatable Moments**: "She missed her daughter's soccer games because she was too exhausted."

**Hope & Action**: End with what they're doing now and why they have hope.

### Step 4: Storytelling Interview (30-60 minutes)

**Interview Questions** (adapt based on patient):

**Background**:

- Tell me about your life before AATD. What did you love to do?
- What were your first symptoms? When did you notice something was wrong?

**Diagnostic Journey**:

- When did you first see a doctor about these symptoms?
- What did they tell you?
- How many doctors did you see before being diagnosed?
- What were you misdiagnosed with?
- How long did this go on?
- How did this impact your life? (work, family, hobbies)

**Emotional Impact**:

- How did you feel during this time? (frustrated, scared, confused)
- What was the hardest part of not having answers?
- Did you ever think about giving up?

**Diagnosis**:

- How did you finally get diagnosed?
- What was your reaction when you found out?
- Looking back, were there signs doctors should have caught earlier?

**Treatment & Life Now**:

- What treatment are you receiving now?
- How has it helped?
- What's life like now compared to before diagnosis?
- What do you wish you'd known earlier?

**Advocacy & Hope**:

- Why did you decide to share your story?
- What do you want people to know about AATD?
- What gives you hope?
- What's your message to someone who's just been diagnosed?

**Close**:

- Is there anything else you'd like to share?
- Any questions for me?

## Ethics & Consent

### Patient Privacy & HIPAA

**Protected Health Information (PHI)** includes:

- Name, address, phone number
- Medical record numbers
- Photos of face
- Any other identifying information

**If patient voluntarily shares their story**: They're waiving their right to privacy **for that specific story only**. Still get written consent.

---

### Informed Consent Requirements

**Patient Storytelling Consent Form** must include:

1. **Purpose**: How story will be used (fundraising, website, advocacy, media)
2. **Content**: What information will be shared (name, photo, diagnosis, story details)
3. **Rights**: Patient can withdraw consent at any time (but published materials may remain)
4. **Compensation**: None (voluntary)
5. **Questions**: Contact person for concerns

**Signature**: Patient signs and dates

**Sample Consent Language**:
_"I, [Name], give Mark Egly Foundation permission to share my AATD story, including my name, photo, and medical information related to AATD, in print materials, online, in media interviews, and in fundraising/advocacy campaigns. I understand I can withdraw consent at any time by contacting [name] at [email], though materials already published may remain in circulation. I have received no compensation for sharing my story."_

---

### Ethical Storytelling Guidelines

**Do**:

- ‚úÖ Get written consent for every use (fundraising, website, social media, etc.)
- ‚úÖ Let patients review and approve before publishing
- ‚úÖ Be transparent about how story will be used
- ‚úÖ Respect patients' boundaries (if they don't want photo used, honor that)
- ‚úÖ Protect minors (parental consent required for anyone under 18)
- ‚úÖ Compensate for time if appropriate (e.g., gift card for 2-hour interview)

**Don't**:

- ‚ùå Share story without consent
- ‚ùå Embellish or change facts
- ‚ùå Use story in ways not disclosed to patient
- ‚ùå Pressure patients to share more than they're comfortable with
- ‚ùå Exploit suffering without showing hope or action

---

### Special Considerations

**Minors**: Parent/guardian must sign consent

**Deceased Patients**: Family member must sign consent (next of kin)

**Sensitive Topics**: If story involves suicide, abuse, or other sensitive topics, consult legal counsel on consent language

**Photos of Patients**: Separate photo release form (some patients may consent to written story but not photo)

## Story Formats

### 1. Written Testimonial (500-1,000 words)

**Structure**:

- Headline (compelling, specific): "After 8 Years and 5 Doctors, Sarah Finally Got Answers"
- Opening hook (emotional moment or shocking detail)
- Background (life before symptoms)
- Diagnostic journey (the struggle)
- Diagnosis and treatment (the turning point)
- Life now and advocacy (the hope)
- Closing quote (powerful, inspiring)

**Tone**: Conversational, emotional, hopeful

**POV**: First-person ("I") or third-person narrative ("Sarah says...")

**Example Opening**:
_"By the time Sarah Martinez was finally diagnosed with AATD, she had been to five different doctors, tried a dozen medications that didn't work, and missed countless family moments because she was too exhausted to participate. 'I felt like I was going crazy,' Sarah says. 'Every doctor told me it was just asthma or stress, but I knew something was seriously wrong.'"_

---

### 2. Video Story (2-5 minutes)

**Format**:

- Patient interviewed in comfortable setting (home, outdoors, support group)
- Mix of interview clips and B-roll (patient doing activities, family photos)
- Captions throughout (accessibility)
- Light background music (hopeful, not overdramatic)
- Opening text: "Patient Name | Diagnosed [Year] | [Location]"
- Closing text: Foundation logo + tagline + website

**B-Roll Ideas**:

- Patient breathing into spirometer or taking medication
- Family photos (before/after diagnosis)
- Patient at work, with family, doing hobbies
- Support group meetings
- Advocacy events

**Interviews**:

- Natural lighting (near window or outdoors)
- Frame patient off-center (rule of thirds)
- Comfortable chair or couch
- Simple background (not cluttered)

---

**Short version of written testimonial**:

- Hook (1-2 sentences)

## Mark's Story

### The Founder's Journey (Template for All Tellings)

**Headline**: _"From Misdiagnosis to Mission: How Mark Egly Turned Personal Frustration into a Foundation"_

### Mark's Story (Full Version - 1,000 words)

**Life Before AATD**

Mark Egly didn't set out to become a rare disease advocate. He was a PGA golf professional, working at country clubs and teaching the sport he loved. Golf was more than a job‚Äîit was his life. He spent hours on the course, walking 18 holes multiple times a week, teaching lessons, and playing in tournaments.

But in his mid-30s, something changed. He started feeling short of breath during rounds. At first, he chalked it up to being out of shape. But even after improving his fitness, the breathlessness persisted. Then came the chronic cough. The fatigue. The chest tightness.

"I knew something was wrong," Mark recalls. "But I didn't know what."

---

**The Medical Run-Around**

Mark did what anyone would do: He went to the doctor. Then another. And another.

Over seven years, Mark saw multiple physicians. Each one had a different theory: asthma, allergies, bronchitis, acid reflux. He tried inhaler after inhaler. Antibiotics. Allergy medications. Nothing worked.

"Every doctor was treating symptoms, not looking for a root cause," Mark says. "I'd leave appointments feeling dismissed, like they thought I was exaggerating."

The worst part wasn't the physical symptoms‚Äîit was the uncertainty. Mark's career depended on being able to walk a golf course. His quality of life depended on breathing freely. And no one could tell him why he couldn't.

As the years went by, his lung function declined. He had to cut back on teaching. Some days, he couldn't make it through 18 holes without stopping to catch his breath. He watched his colleagues and friends play effortlessly while he struggled.

"I felt like my body was betraying me, and no one could explain why."

---

**The Self-Diagnosis**

By year seven, Mark was frustrated enough to take matters into his own hands. He started researching his symptoms online‚Äîshortness of breath, early-onset emphysema, chronic lung disease.

That's when he came across Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency (AATD).

The description matched perfectly: genetic lung disease, often misdiagnosed as asthma or COPD, affects people in their 30s-50s. A simple blood test can diagnose it.

"I read about AATD and thought, 'This is me. This is what I have,'" Mark says. "But why hadn't any of my doctors tested for it?"

He brought his research to his doctor and asked for an AAT blood test. The doctor was skeptical but agreed.

Two weeks later, the results confirmed it: Mark had Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency, severe type (ZZ genotype). The diagnosis that should have taken one blood test had taken seven years.

---

**The Turning Point**

Mark's emotions were mixed. Relief‚Äîfinally, an answer. Anger‚Äîthis could have been diagnosed years ago. Fear‚Äîwhat damage had already been done?

He started augmentation therapy (weekly infusions of AAT protein) to slow the progression of his lung disease. He changed his lifestyle, avoiding lung irritants and optimizing his health. Slowly, he regained some quality of life.

But the question haunted him: **Why did it take seven years?**

The more Mark learned about AATD, the more alarmed he became. His experience wasn't unique‚Äîit was typical. The average AATD patient waits 7-8 years and sees 4+ doctors before diagnosis. An estimated 300,000 Americans have AATD but don't know it. Diagnostic delays lead to irreversible lung damage that could have been prevented.

"I thought, 'This is insane. We know what AATD is. We know how to test for it. We know how to treat it. So why are people waiting years for answers?'"

---

**From Frustration to Innovation**

Mark isn't someone who accepts broken systems. As a businessman and golfer, he's used to problem-solving, strategizing, and innovating. He started thinking: What if we could diagnose AATD faster? What if we could repurpose the AAT protein for other conditions?

That curiosity led Mark to research and eventually secure a patent (US20220160847) for repurposing AAT protein to help firefighters, military personnel, and asthma patients‚Äîgroups at higher risk of lung damage.

But the patent was just the beginning. Mark realized that fixing the AATD diagnostic delay crisis required a comprehensive approach: educating doctors, supporting patients, funding research, and changing policy.

So in 2025, he founded **Mark Egly Foundation** with a bold mission: **Eliminate AATD diagnostic delays.**

---

**The Mission Today**

Mark Egly Foundation isn't like other rare disease organizations. It's laser-focused on one problem: diagnostic delays. The foundation works on four fronts:

1. **Medical Education**: Training physicians to recognize AATD early through free CME courses
2. **Patient Support**: Helping patients navigate testing, treatment, and the healthcare system
3. **Research Innovation**: Funding quick-test devices, mass AAT production, and novel therapies
4. **Policy Advocacy**: Pushing for newborn screening and workplace protections

The goal is ambitious but measurable: **Cut diagnostic delays in half‚Äîfrom 7-8 years to 3-4 years‚Äîby 2030.**

"If I can save even one person from going through what I went through, this foundation will have been worth it," Mark says. "But I think we can help thousands."

---

**Mark's Message**

Today, Mark is stable on augmentation therapy. He's back on the golf course, though not at the pace he once was. But his focus has shifted from par scores to policy wins, from teaching golf to teaching doctors about AATD.

His message to AATD patients: **"You're not alone. There's hope. And there's treatment‚Äîif you can get diagnosed."**

His message to doctors: **"Think AATD. Test early. One blood test can change‚Äîor save‚Äîa life."**

His message to supporters: **"We can fix this. Diagnostic delays are preventable. With your help, we'll make sure no one else waits seven years for answers."**

### Example Case Study

**Title**: _From 8-Year Delay to 3-Month Diagnosis: How Patient Navigation Changed Sarah's Life_

---

**Background**
Sarah Martinez, a 42-year-old elementary school teacher from Phoenix, experienced shortness of breath and chronic cough for eight years before being diagnosed with AATD. By the time she found Mark Egly Foundation, she had seen six doctors, tried twelve medications, and lost hope of ever getting answers.

---

**The Challenge**
Sarah's symptoms began at age 34. Over the next eight years, she was misdiagnosed with asthma, anxiety, and COPD. No treatment worked. Her lung function declined. She missed work frequently and couldn't keep up with her students or her own children.

"I felt like I was drowning," Sarah says. "And no one could tell me why."

By age 42, Sarah had nearly given up. Then a friend shared a social media post about AATD. Sarah reached out to Mark Egly Foundation's patient navigation program.

---

**The Intervention**
Sarah was paired with a patient navigator, who:

1. Reviewed Sarah's medical history and symptoms
2. Connected Sarah with a pulmonologist experienced in AATD
3. Helped Sarah obtain an AAT blood test (covered by insurance)
4. Coordinated follow-up genetic testing to confirm diagnosis
5. Assisted with insurance approval for augmentation therapy

Total time from first contact to diagnosis: **3 months**.

---

**The Outcome**
Sarah was diagnosed with severe AATD (ZZ genotype) and started augmentation therapy. Within six months, her quality of life improved significantly.

"For the first time in eight years, I can breathe," Sarah says. "I'm back to teaching full-time. I can play with my kids. I have my life back."

Sarah also joined a support group and now volunteers as a patient storyteller and advocate.

---

**The Impact**
Sarah's case demonstrates the power of patient navigation:

- **Speed**: 3 months vs. 8 years (97% faster)
- **Accuracy**: Direct path to correct diagnosis (no more misdiagnoses)
- **Access**: Navigator helped overcome insurance and referral barriers
- **Support**: Sarah wasn't alone in navigating the system

Mark Egly Foundation is scaling this model to serve 500 patients per year by Year 5.

---

**Key Takeaways**

- ‚úÖ Patient navigation dramatically reduces diagnostic delays
- ‚úÖ Connecting patients with AATD-experienced physicians is critical
- ‚úÖ Insurance navigation removes financial barriers
