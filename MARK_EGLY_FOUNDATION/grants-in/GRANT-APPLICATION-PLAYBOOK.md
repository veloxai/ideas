## Grant Writing Principles

### The Foundation of Grant Success

**Principle 1: Alignment is Everything**

- Your project must align with funder's priorities (not just your needs)
- Read guidelines carefully, use funder's language
- Show how your work advances their mission

**Principle 2: Tell a Compelling Story**

- Start with the problem (human impact, urgency)
- Show why your solution is unique and effective
- Demonstrate measurable outcomes

**Principle 3: Demonstrate Credibility**

- Prove your organization can deliver (track record, expertise)
- Show sound financial management
- Include strong leadership and governance

**Principle 4: Be Specific and Concrete**

- Vague = weak; specific = strong
- Use data, examples, stories
- Clear metrics and evaluation plan

**Principle 5: Make it Easy to Say Yes**

- Organize clearly (use headings, bullets)
- Follow instructions exactly (page limits, font, format)
- Write clearly (no jargon, active voice)
- Answer all questions asked

## Pre-Application Research

### Step 1: Know Your Funder

**Research Questions**:

- What is funder's mission and priorities?
- What types of projects do they fund?
- What geographic areas? What population groups?
- What is typical grant size and duration?
- What are they NOT interested in funding?

**Where to Find Info**:

- Funder website (grant guidelines, past awards)
- Annual reports and IRS Form 990 (foundation tax return, shows all grants)
- News articles and press releases
- Candid/Foundation Directory (foundation profiles)

---

### Step 2: Review Past Grants

**Look for patterns**:

- Who received funding? (organization type, size, location)
- Project types funded (research, education, direct service)
- Grant amounts (range, average)
- Multi-year grants or one-time?
- Any repeat grantees? (funder values long-term relationships)

**Ask yourself**: Are we similar to past grantees? If not, why would we be funded?

---

### Step 3: Contact Program Officer (If Allowed)

**When to reach out**:

- Before LOI or full proposal (not after deadline)
- If guidelines unclear
- If want to confirm fit before investing time

**How to approach**:

- Email (concise, professional)
- Phone call (if funder prefers, shorter)
- In-person meeting (if local, after email introduction)

**What to ask**:

- "We're considering applying for [grant]. Does our project align with your priorities?"
- "Would you recommend we apply, or wait for a better fit?"
- "Any advice for our application?"
- "What makes a strong application in your view?"

**What NOT to do**:

- Ask them to review your draft proposal (unless they offer)
- Call multiple times
- Be pushy or entitled

**Note**: Some funders don't allow pre-application contact. Respect that.

---

### Step 4: Assess Your Fit

**Questions to ask**:

- Mission alignment: Does our project advance their goals? (90%+ match)
- Eligibility: Do we meet all requirements? (geography, organization type, project type)
- Funding level: Is grant size appropriate for our project?
- Capacity: Can we execute this project successfully?
- Competitive: How many applications will they receive? What's our edge?
- ROI: Is time investment worth potential return?

**Decision Rule**: Only proceed if 4+ of 6 questions = YES

## Proposal Components

### 1. Executive Summary / Cover Letter

**Purpose**: Hook the reader, summarize key points

**Length**: 1 page (or as specified)

**What to include**:

- Opening: Compelling statement (problem, urgency, human impact)
- Who you are: Organization name, mission, credibility
- The problem: Brief overview (data, why it matters)
- Your solution: Project summary (what, who, where, when)
- Funding request: Amount requested, project duration
- Impact: Expected outcomes (measurable, concrete)
- Closing: Thank them for considering, offer to provide more info

**Tone**: Confident but not arrogant, passionate but not emotional

**Example Opening**:

> "Every year, 100,000 Americans live with undiagnosed Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency (AATD), suffering through an average 7-8 year diagnostic odyssey. The Mark Egly Foundation is changing this reality through physician education, patient support, and innovative research‚Äîand we need your partnership to accelerate our impact."

---

### 2. Organizational Background

**Purpose**: Establish credibility and capacity

**Length**: 1-2 pages

**What to include**:

- **Mission and vision**: Why we exist
- **History**: When founded, by whom, key milestones
- **Programs and services**: What we do (brief overview)
- **Impact to date**: Key accomplishments (numbers, stories)
- **Leadership**: Board, executive team (credentials, diversity)
- **Financials**: Budget size, funding sources (diversified), financial health
- **Unique strengths**: What sets us apart (founder's story, patient-centered approach)

**Tips**:

- Tailor to funder (emphasize aspects relevant to their priorities)
- Use data (patients served, physicians trained, research funded)
- Include testimonials or brief quotes (board member, patient, partner)

---

### 3. Problem Statement / Need

**Purpose**: Make the case for why this project is needed

**Length**: 1-3 pages

**What to include**:

- **The problem**: What's wrong, who's affected, scope/scale
- **Evidence**: Data, statistics, research (cite sources)
- **Urgency**: Why now? What happens if we don't act?
- **Gap**: What's missing in current landscape?
- **Who's affected**: Target population (demographics, geography)
- **Root causes**: Why does this problem exist?
- **Connection to funder**: How does this align with their priorities?

**Structure**:

1. Hook: Start with compelling story or statistic
2. Scope: Quantify the problem (national, local)
3. Impact: Show human and societal cost
4. Gap: Explain why existing solutions inadequate
5. Bridge: Lead into your solution (next section)

**Example**:

> "AATD affects 1 in 2,500 Americans, yet only 10% are diagnosed. The average patient sees 4+ doctors and waits 7-8 years before diagnosis‚Äîyears in which lung damage becomes irreversible. Why? 90% of primary care physicians report they 'rarely or never' think to test for AATD (Source: ATS Survey 2023). This diagnostic delay costs lives and billions in preventable healthcare spending."

---

### 4. Project Description / Methodology

**Purpose**: Explain your solution in detail

**Length**: 3-5 pages (most substantial section)

**What to include**:

**A. Goals and Objectives**:

- **Goal**: Broad, long-term outcome (e.g., "Reduce AATD diagnostic delays")
- **Objectives**: Specific, measurable, time-bound (SMART)
  - Example: "Train 500 primary care physicians in AATD recognition by December 2027"

**B. Target Population**:

- Who will benefit? (patients, physicians, communities)
- How many? (numbers)
- Selection criteria (if applicable)

**C. Activities / Methods**:

- What will you DO? (step-by-step)
- Use timeline or Gantt chart (visual)
- Assign responsibilities (who does what)

**D. Staffing**:

- Who will lead and implement? (qualifications)
- Time commitment (FTE)

**E. Partners / Collaborators**:

- Any partner organizations?
- Letters of commitment (include as attachment)

**F. Innovation**:

- What's new or unique about your approach?
- How is this different from what already exists?

**G. Barriers and Mitigation**:

- What could go wrong?
- How will you address challenges?

**Structure**:

- Use subheadings (Goals, Activities, Timeline, etc.)
- Bullet points and tables (easier to scan)
- Be specific (not vague generalities)

**Example Activity Description**:

> **Activity 1: CME Course Development (Months 1-3)**
> We will create a 1-hour online CME course, "AATD: Early Detection, Better Outcomes." The course will include:
>
> - 5 video modules (10-12 minutes each) featuring AATD specialists
> - Case studies and interactive quizzes
> - Downloadable patient handouts
> - CME credits through joint providership with [Institution]
>
> **Lead**: Medical Education Director (0.5 FTE)
> **Partners**: Johns Hopkins (CME accreditation), 3 AATD physicians (content experts)
> **Deliverable**: Accredited CME course live on MarkEglyFoundation.org by Month 3

---

### 5. Evaluation Plan

**Purpose**: Show how you'll measure success

**Length**: 1-2 pages

**What to include**:

**A. Outcomes / Impact**:

- What will change as a result of your project?
- Short-term outcomes (knowledge, attitudes, skills)
- Long-term outcomes (behavior change, systems change, health outcomes)

**B. Metrics / Indicators**:

- How will you measure outcomes? (specific metrics)
- Quantitative (numbers): Enrollment, completion rates, test results
- Qualitative (stories): Interviews, surveys, case studies

**C. Data Collection**:

- How will you collect data? (surveys, tests, tracking systems)
- When? (baseline, midpoint, end)
- Who will collect? (staff, external evaluator)

**D. Analysis**:

- How will you analyze data?
- Statistical methods (if applicable)
- Comparison (before/after, treatment/control)

**E. Reporting**:

- How will you share findings?
- Internal reports (board, staff)
- External reports (funder, stakeholders, public)

**Example Evaluation Plan**:

> **Objective**: Train 500 primary care physicians in AATD recognition by December 2027
>
> **Metrics**:
>
> - Enrollment: # physicians who register for CME course
> - Completion: % who complete all 5 modules
> - Knowledge gain: Pre-test vs. post-test scores (% increase)
> - Behavior change: # physicians who report testing ‚â•1 patient for AATD within 6 months (6-month follow-up survey)
> - Impact: # AATD diagnoses attributed to trained physicians (patient tracking)
>
> **Data Collection**:
>
> - LMS (Learning Management System) tracks enrollment, completion, test scores
> - Email survey at 6 months (SurveyMonkey)
> - Patient tracking form (physicians report diagnoses)
>
> **Analysis**: Descriptive statistics (averages, percentages); compare to control group (physicians who didn't take course)
>
> **Reporting**: Quarterly reports to funder; annual public report on website

### 7. Sustainability Plan

**Purpose**: Show project will continue after grant ends

**Length**: 1 paragraph to 1 page

**What to include**:

- How will project be funded after grant period?

## Writing Tips & Best Practices

### Clarity and Conciseness

‚úÖ **DO**:

- Use short sentences (15-20 words max)
- Use active voice ("We will train physicians" not "Physicians will be trained")
- Use concrete, specific language ("500 physicians" not "many physicians")
- Define acronyms on first use (AATD, CME, etc.)
- Use subheadings, bullets, white space (easier to scan)

‚ùå **DON'T**:

- Use jargon or technical language (unless audience expects it)
- Use passive voice
- Use vague language ("substantial," "significant," "numerous")
- Write long, dense paragraphs (3-5 sentences max per paragraph)

---

### Evidence and Data

‚úÖ **DO**:

- Cite sources for statistics (ATS, CDC, published research)
- Use recent data (within 5 years)
- Include patient stories (with permission, anonymized)
- Show your impact data (if available)

‚ùå **DON'T**:

- Make unsupported claims
- Use outdated data
- Rely only on anecdotes (balance with statistics)

---

### Tone

‚úÖ **DO**:

- Be confident (not arrogant)
- Be passionate (not emotional/manipulative)
- Be professional (not stuffy)
- Show urgency (not desperation)

‚ùå **DON'T**:

- Beg or plead
- Be overly emotional
- Criticize others (other organizations, government, etc.)
- Make unrealistic promises

---

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå **Mistake 1**: Not following instructions

- Read guidelines carefully, follow exactly (font, margins, page limits, attachments)
- Answer every question asked
- Submit by deadline (preferably 2 days early)

‚ùå **Mistake 2**: Writing about your organization (not the funder's priorities)

- Lead with problem/need (not organization history)
- Show alignment with funder's mission
- Use funder's language (from guidelines, website)

‚ùå **Mistake 3**: Vague or unmeasurable objectives

- Bad: "Increase awareness of AATD"
- Good: "Train 500 physicians in AATD recognition, resulting in 20% increase in testing rates by December 2027"

‚ùå **Mistake 4**: Weak evaluation plan

- Don't just track activities (# workshops held)
- Track outcomes (knowledge gain, behavior change, health outcomes)
- Be realistic (don't promise to cure AATD in 1 year)

‚ùå **Mistake 5**: Budget errors

- Math errors (rows don't add up)
- Unallowable expenses (lobbying, political activities, alcohol)
- Insufficient budget narrative (explain line items)
- Budget doesn't match proposal (activities in narrative not budgeted)

## Budget Development

### Budget Components

**Personnel** (Salaries and Benefits):

- Staff salaries (list each position, % FTE, salary, % charged to grant)
- Fringe benefits (typically 25-35% of salaries)
- Consultants (contractors, external experts)

**Non-Personnel**:

- Travel (conferences, site visits, mileage)
- Supplies and materials (office supplies, educational materials)
- Equipment (computers, printers, furniture‚Äîif over $5K threshold)
- Space and occupancy (rent, utilities, phone/internet)
- Other direct costs (printing, postage, insurance, software licenses)
- Indirect costs (overhead, admin costs‚Äîif allowed by funder)

**Matching Funds** (if required):

- Cash match (money from other sources)
- In-kind match (donated goods, volunteer time, space)

---

### Budget Narrative

**For each line item, explain**:

- What it is
- Why it's necessary
- How calculated (formula)

**Example**:

> **Program Director (0.5 FTE, $70,000 annual salary): $35,000**
> The Program Director will oversee CME course development, manage partnerships with medical schools, and supervise course implementation. 50% of time dedicated to this project. Salary based on market rate for similar positions in healthcare nonprofits (Source: GuideStar Nonprofit Compensation Report).

---

### Budget Best Practices

‚úÖ **DO**:

- Be realistic (not inflated, not bare bones)
- Align budget with activities (if you say you'll hire staff, budget for it)
- Include all costs (don't forget indirect costs, benefits, small items)
- Show match or cost-sharing (if applicable, demonstrates commitment)

‚ùå **DON'T**:

- Include unallowable costs (check funder guidelines)
- Pad budget (inflated costs)
- Submit budget with math errors
- Forget budget narrative (explain every line)

## Common Attachments

### Standard Attachments (Almost Always Required)

1. **IRS 501(c)(3) Determination Letter**
   - Proves tax-exempt status
   - PDF of official IRS letter

2. **Financial Statements**
   - Most recent audited financials (balance sheet, income statement, cash flow)
   - Or, if budget <$250K: IRS Form 990
   - Shows financial health

3. **Organizational Budget**
   - Current year operating budget (all programs, not just this project)
   - Shows revenue sources and expenses

4. **Board of Directors List**
   - Names, affiliations, positions
   - Shows governance and diversity

5. **Letters of Support or Commitment**
   - From partners, collaborators, beneficiaries
   - Confirms buy-in and feasibility

9. **Timeline or Gantt Chart**
10. **Data Use Agreement or MOU** (if partnering with other organizations)
11. **IRB Approval** (if research involving human subjects)
12. **Photos or Videos** (patient stories, program activities)

### Attachment Tips

‚úÖ **DO**:

- Label clearly (filename: "MarkEglyFoundation_501c3Letter.pdf")
- Follow funder's file format requirements (PDF, Word, size limits)
- Ensure documents are current (within 1-2 years for financials)

‚ùå **DON'T**:

- Send unsolicited attachments (only what's requested)
- Use generic templates (customize for this funder)

## Review & Quality Control

### Internal Review Process (Before Submission)

**Step 1: Self-Review** (Grant Writer):

- Read entire proposal aloud (catches awkward phrasing, typos)
- Check all questions answered
- Verify budget math
- Run spell-check and grammar-check

**Step 2: Program Staff Review** (Content Experts):

- Verify accuracy of program details
- Ensure data and citations correct
- Confirm activities and timeline realistic

**Step 3: CDO Review** (Chief Development Officer):

- Check alignment with funder priorities
- Ensure strong case made
- Review budget for accuracy and appropriateness
- Check attachments complete and correct

**Step 4: Executive Director Review** (ED):

- Sign off on proposal (ED signature required)
- Review budget and commitments
- Ensure organization can deliver

**Step 5: External Review** (Optional, for large grants):

- Peer review (colleague at another nonprofit)
- Consultant review (grant writing expert)
- Board member review (Development Committee chair)

**Timeline**: Allow 1 week for internal review process

## Post-Submission Follow-Up

### Week 1: Confirm Receipt

**Action**: Email program officer

- "I submitted our proposal for [Grant Name] on [Date]. Can you confirm receipt?"
- "Please let me know if you need any additional information."

**If no response**: Check funder website (some send auto-confirmation), check portal status

---

### Week 2-4: Offer to Answer Questions

**Action**: Brief email

- "As you review our proposal, please don't hesitate to reach out with questions."
- "I'm happy to provide any clarifying information."

**Don't**: Pester with multiple emails (one follow-up is enough)

---

### Decision Notification

**If Awarded** üéâ:

- Thank program officer (phone call + email)
- Review grant agreement carefully (budget, reporting, timeline)
- Sign and return promptly
- Announce internally (staff, board) and externally (press release, social media)
- Update grant tracking database (status: Awarded)
- Set up project files and reporting calendar

**If Declined** üòû:

- Thank program officer for consideration
- Request feedback ("What could we improve?")
- Ask if reapplication encouraged (some funders say "try again next year")
- Update grant tracking database (status: Declined, notes: feedback)
- Learn and improve (adjust proposal for next submission)

**If Waitlisted**:

- Stay engaged (check in monthly for updates)
- Be ready to answer additional questions
- Patience (sometimes waitlist converts to award)
